FLUX #004 - MyTransport.SG

28 November 2019 (First published on 8kguy.com)

When it comes to bus timing apps, we have many options here in Singapore. From SG Buses to SG Dr Bus, they all do the same thing. But in this FLUX, we will only focus on one particular app: MyTransport.SG. Yep, this is the all-in-one transport app by Land Transport Authority.

Issue #1: Waste of Space

The home screen has two tabs, one for bus or MRT, as well as one for driving. Tapping on each tab will show their respective information. However, the search bar on top is only for buses, no matter which tab you are on. Why is it still shown when I am on the driving tab?

Also, look at that yellow semi-circle at the bottom. Why does it have to be so big? It is taking up space that can be used for more content. Don’t just here it from me; Look at this review from the Play Store.

Issue #2: Unnecessarily Long Menu

The biggest issue with this app is the menu. While the list is mostly well sorted, it is just too long. And a long list means users have to read through a lot of words to find what they want.

A deeper look reveals some items in this list are not very useful. For example, “Cycling Routes” shows a few route but doesn’t provide any info about each of them. It also has a draw-your-own-route feature, but doesn’t snap to any grid so the estimated distance is inaccurate.

“Snap & Send” only lets you report road defects. Meanwhile, if you want to report PMDs, you will have to go to “Report PMD/PAB Incidents”. Very misleading. Why not merge it with “Snap & Send”?

Also, most of the items in the menu are accessible from the home screen. Why is there a need for repetition?

Issue #3: Lack of Flexibility & Clutter

In this app, you can learn more about MRT fares and stations. There is a map, as well as a list of stations. However, the map can only calculate fare and duration. To find out more about each station, you have to use the list. Not ideal for people that do not remember stations by their names.

Another issue with the MRT feature is that the bicycle lots icons get in the way, whenever present. As seen from the screenshot below, we are unable to see where exit D is, as it is covered by one of those icons.

A New Way to Navigate

Due to the amount of features in this app, we need a new way to navigate the app. I opted to go with a tab-style menu, like what you will find on Facebook. Each tab handles a mode of transportation, with one tab just for reporting. Since most users of this app use it for bus information, I made it the main tab.

Other tabs are arranged in order of probability of being used, from MRT to bicycle. The “report” tab is located on the left of the bus tab, to allow users to quickly switch to it. This should encourage more users to use it. You can now report both PMD and road defect issues from this one tab.

Oh, I threw “About Us” here too as nobody ever reads it.

The “bus” tab houses all features relating to bus. I placed the favorite bus stops list below everything else. So even when this list gets long, you do not need to scroll down to see everything else.

At the very bottom is the search bar. I placed it here as it is easy to reach, and also for the sake of consistency. You will see what I mean in a while, when we look at the other tabs.

Improved MRT Interaction

By adding a toggle that switches between calculator and station mode, users can do both from the same map. For those that prefer to search by name, we have the search bar at the bottom.

Station Mode

Calculator Mode

With a movable map, how are you going to swipe left/right to switch tabs? Swipe the fade out part of the screen below, where the search bar is located. Not the most ideal solution, but this is the best I can do.

Since the bicycle parking icons are a little intrusive, I removed them from the station info. Cyclists can still see all the bicycle parking information under the “bicycle” tab.

Drive & Bicycle Tabs

Like “bus” tab, “drive” tab also contains an expandable list: Traffic news. Unlike bus timings though, I placed this list at the top of the page. As long as there isn’t many accidents happening concurrently, the user would not have to scroll the list. All the toggles can be found below, where they are easy to reach.

Meanwhile, the “bicycle” tab houses what used to be the “Bicycle Parking”. I brought down the search bar, so it is easy to reach, as well as for consistency. Otherwise, nothing I would change.
