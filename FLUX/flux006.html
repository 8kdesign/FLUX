FLUX #006 - POKET Rewards

20 December 2019 (First published on 8kguy.com)

Poket was a popular reward app with multiple big clients. It was. These days though, it is hard to come by a shop that uses it. A look at the app, and you will understand why. The app industry has evolved, but Poket clearly hasn’t caught up. In this article, let’s give it a long-overdue makeover.

Issue #1: Unpolished Layout

Back then, app developers paid little attention to how users feel using the app. It just had to work. A lot has changed since then though. Let’s begin with the layout of the app.

First thing you see when you open Poket is not the reward cards or vouchers, but toggles to add cards. The purpose of this app is not to collect reward cards; it is to use them. So why are the cards and vouchers not the first thing you see? Also, housing only 3 toggles on the main page is a huge waste of space.

Next, let’s talk about the “More” tab, which is just a lazy way to dump everything else. But there are potentially useful features here that users might neglect because they are thrown here. An example of this would be the history log, a fast and easy way for users to check if they received points.

Issue #2: Dated Design

Now, let’s talk about the looks of the app. Having a “welcome” sign in a squiggly font on the main page is so 2012. The background image on this page is also so bizarre in an era where simplicity is king.

Next, the cards list looks horrible. Their designs lack coherence, and most look like they were made by primary school kids. Also, that frame around each card is really unnecessary.

I know the cards are designed by the clients, but they do leave a bad impression on the users. Can’t Poket come up with a template and guideline to make them look good?

Issue #3: Confusing QR Code System

Despite being under one account, you have a different QR code for every single card. There is also one QR code for the account itself. Why is there a need for so many QR codes? It may sound insignificant, but it does bring about a couple of issues and loads of confusions for the user.

Firstly, it means that you need to search for the right card, increasing interactions required before you can scan. Secondly, it takes up additional space on the card information page. It also leads to questions like “How do I tell if it is the correct card?”, since some stores have multiple cards.

Issue #4: Bad Search Results

There are multiple ways to add a card: “search by merchant name”, “scan merchant QR code” or “let merchant scan my QR code”. My concern lies with the first.

The merchant name isn’t always the store/brand name. For example, the Collin’s card doesn’t appear when you search “Collin’s”. Instead, since it is created by Awecent ParTea, it appears when “tea” is searched. In some cases, the card isn’t even contain the store branding, like EIN’s Mi Store card.

Concept by 8K

New Looks & A Unified QR Code

For this concept, I wanted to bring the feel of luxury into the app. The goal is to create an experience so good that drives the user to actively use this app. We want customers to feel good no matter where they are buying from. My first step was to replace the turquoise accent with black, the color that conveys prestige.

I initially made the cards page what you see upon opening the app. But with a unified QR code, the focus is now on this QR code, so I made the “Me” page the main page. You can see your avatar on this page, so you can be sure that you are logged in to the right account. Surely you don’t want the points to go to others.

With this unified QR code, you can scan for points from all supported stores with just one QR code. Each voucher still has its own code though, for obvious reasons. Here, you can also edit your profile.

Coherent Card Design

Ensuring quality of design of cards is not easy. But to ensure that card designs are consistently, all we need is a template. I came up with a pretty basic one, as seen below. Since most physical reward cards are credit card sized, cards in the app has the same familiar aspect ratio.

It can be hard to find the card you need when you have a ton of them. I thought it would be good if we can sort the cards by category, like F&B or shopping for example.

I also created a template for vouchers. It has some elements in common with the card template. But instead of looking like a credit card, I shaped it like paper shopping vouchers.

Just like before, you can swipe left from the cards page to see the vouchers.

Better Way to Add Cards

The original app wasted a lot of space with the add card toggles. For my concept, I placed a “+” icon at the bottom of the cards page. Tap on this to open the search overlay. In the original, you had to search cards by merchant name. To fix this, the ability to also search by card name can be added.

I removed the QR code options as they did not make a lot of sense. Stores do not openly display their QR code and we don’t want people joining the cashier queue just to add a card.

New Card Info Page

With a unified QR code, there isn’t a need for QR code in the card info page. The switching of menu bar below irked me, so I went for a scrolling page instead. Things that users are most likely to refer to, like card expiry and point redemption, are placed towards the top, to reduce scrolling.

Despite taking up a lot of space, it didn’t feel right to remove the image of the card from the page. In a way, this card is the page’s identity. Instead, I decided to make it more useful. You can now refer to the card to find out the tier of your card, and the category it belongs in.

Location list is less important since it is readily available on Google, so it put it lower. I added store images, so users can refer to it and see if the store is worth visiting. At the very end of the list is the delete button. I placed it here to reduce the chances of users accidentally clicking on it and as a result, panic.

Settings Page, History Log & Notification

As mentioned earlier, history log has the potential to be frequently used. Hence, I shifted it to the menu bar.

To make space in the menu bar, I shifted the inbox (notifications) to the header. It is now an overlay, just like the search bar. This reduces number of proper pages, so the app feels less bloated.

With “history log” and “edit profile” moved, the “more” page is now simpler. I also got rid of “tell your friends” since it is unlikely anyone will ever use it. The name “more” is ambiguous, so I renamed it to settings.
