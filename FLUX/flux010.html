FLUX #010 - FuntouchOS Settings

5 March 2020 (First published on 8kguy.com)

TLDR: My concept simplifies the FuntouchOS settings app. Click here to experience it for yourself!

If you are using a Vivo device right now, you will know how poorly organized the settings app is. Not only is it ridiculously long , things are often not where you expect them to be. Thankfully, there is a search bar. But that doesn’t mean we should settle with something so bad.

Thus I decided to take up the challenge, to reorganize the FuntouchOS settings app. After hours of planning and even more on designing, it is finally time to share my concept.

Issue #1: Too Much Reading

Before we begin, let’s take a look at the main issues I had with the settings app. Despite being super long, the main page couldn’t contain everything. So Vivo added a “More settings” page. The problem with this though, is that there isn’t a clear indication of what is inside this “More settings” page.

What this meant is that I often have to look through 2 long lists, before finding what I need. As a result, using the search bar has become a norm for me. Surely I am not the only one facing this issue.

Issue #2: Disorganized Mess

Also mentioned earlier, the app is so messy that things are often where they don’t belong.

Here is an example: To enable smart panel on the home screen, you would go to “Home screen, lock screen and wallpaper” > “Home screen settings” and enable “Smart Launcher”. But to change the content in this smart panel, you need to go to “Jovi” > “Smart Scene” and change from there.

Why can’t I enable and edit the smart panel on the same page? Also, the term “Jovi” is meaningless to consumers. It doesn’t make sense to use it as a category.

There were also parts that appears multiple time, even in the same section. On “Backup & Reset” page, “Backup data” > “vivoCloud” and “Restore data” > ‘Recover from vivoCloud” brings you to the same page.

Issue #3: iManager Overlapping Features

Vivo has an app called iManager where you can manage your device. However, a lot of its features overlap with the settings app. And among those that don’t, most of them would make sense in the settings app. In other words, there is really no point in having this app. It needs to be merged.

For example, its “Space cleanup” feature can be accessed through the settings app. Same thing for “Ultra Game Mode”, “Data monitor” and “Battery Management”. For some reason, the “App Manager” is different for both apps. Meanwhile, things like “Screen usage time” aren’t in the settings app when they should be.

Issue #4: Unnecessary Interactions

From mapping out the entire settings app, I found out that users have to go through up to 8 layers to reach every aspect of the app. That is a lot of layers. More layers means more reading and tapping. In my concept, I will limit it to just 6. And commonly used toggles will be accessible within the first 3 layers.

Example of 7 & 8 layers:
1) main page > “Jovi” > “Smart Scene” > “More settings” > “Help” > “More Questions” > FAQ question
2) main page > “More settings” > “Accessibility” > “Select to Speak” > “Settings” > “Text to speech settings” > “Preferred engine” > Selection page

At various portions of the settings app, you have the option to check for update for that individual portion. I find that to be rather annoying. Why can’t the app do those updates on its own? Also, how often are they expecting an update for those? Why not push it with the firmware update?

Issue #5: Incoherence in Design

Some sections of the settings app’s UI look very different from others, especially those that are part of iManager. The worse offender though, has to be the Ultra Game Mode, which looks totally out of place.

Concept by 8K

Shortened Main Page

With my concept, I have shortened the very first page you see. On a device like the NEX 3, the entire page is less than 2 screens long. Compare that to the 2.5 (main page) + 2 (more settings) screens long menu on the original app. Additonally, I merged the iManager app with into it as well.

How did I do it? I introduced 2 new categories: “Customization” and “Shortcut & Features”. With them, I was able to sort many options that were scattered across the app. Not only does this shorten the list, it helps users locate these toggles with much greater ease.

Toggles & Interactions

Another change you might have noticed are the toggles for Wi-Fi, mobile data and OTG. I wanted users to be able to toggle them right from the main page. But they will still have to access those pages for other settings. To do so, users can tap anywhere from the words to the arrow on the left of the toggle.

For some options, like OTG, you see “?” instead of “>”. When you tap on it, instead of bringing you to another page, a popup appears at the bottom of the screen. It stays until the user taps the screen. Why popups? Unlike pages, they give users an illusion that they are still on the same layer.

Except those on the main page or those with arrows on their right, tapping on options with text toggles also brings up popups. Just like they are now. Those with arrows on their right opens a new page.

Layout & Coherence

Every page of the app has a similar layout. For those with toggles on the main page, the same toggle is found at the top of the page. Portions of the page that are expandable are positioned at the bottom. This allows other toggles to stay at the top, no matter how long the expandable list is.

Some pages have been merged, to make better use of space. That in turn contributes to a shorter menu. An example of this would be “Font Style” and “Font Sizes” page, now merged into a “Font” page.

Ultra Game Mode now has a UI that matches the entire app.

Bonus: Merger of Shortcut Center & Recent Apps

If you play around with my concept, you might notice something missing. I did not include settings for control center, for a good reason. Currently, there are 2 ways to manage apps that are running: recent apps and the control centre. On both, you can switch or close apps in the background.

Why not just merge them into one? So that is what I did with this concept. The first thing you will see on the new recent apps are the apps running, as well as the most commonly used shortcuts. From here, a swipe up from the bottom reveals the rest of the shortcuts.
