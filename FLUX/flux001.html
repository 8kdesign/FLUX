FLUX #001 - Redmart Lazada

8 October 2019 (First published on 8kguy.com)

Before I begin, here is the story behind this concept. It was actually RedMart’s job posting for a UX designer that inspired me to make it. Being a little curious, I checked out RedMart on the Lazada app. The moment I saw its UI, I was like: “Uggh! Who thought this was a good idea?”.

Issue #1: Wrong Approach

Layout-wise, RedMart’s UI is a lot like Lazada’s app, with lists where the items have little in common. As an ecommerce platform with a wide range of gadgets, clothing and more, I can understand why Lazada would do that. But this strategy isn’t ideal for an online supermarket service.

Why? Unlike clothing or tech gadgets, groceries are a necessity that needs to be topped up weekly. People already have what they want to get in mind before they open RedMart. They aren’t going to search a long list of random items for what they want. What they need is an efficient way to find those items.

Both its mobile and desktop UI are designed in a way that draws the user’s attention towards the featured product lists. On the desktop version, the category menu is a wordy list on the left. Compare that to the featured products list with large colorful images and it is clear which the audience will see first.

Almost none of these products are things users would get just because of discount. To make matters worse, many of them are not even on discount. Not only are you wasting space on things that your users may not be interested in, you are wasting their time. User’s attention span is limited, so make good use of it.

Meanwhile, the mobile interface is better, but not by much. Each category is accompanied by an icon, but the icons are so small and boring that the first thing users see would still be on the featured products list.

The process of shopping groceries online should not be too different from going to a supermarket. Searching by category allows users to go through their shopping list with ease. At the same time, they can also compare prices across brands. The categories menu should be the first thing that catches their attention.

Issue #2: Poor Utilization of Banner Space

The Lazada-style main banner takes a huge amount of screen-estate. It occupies almost half of usable space on the desktop version and a good quarter on the mobile version. But what is it used for?

Currently, it shows pages for ambiguous lists of discounted products, like what you would find on Lazada. But as mentioned earlier, this type of list isn’t ideal. Shoppers love discount, but the discount should come to them, not the other way round. They shouldn’t have to hurt their eyes hunting for the best deal.

Meanwhile, there are these small banners that appear between featured product lists. Some of these banners show deals like get free gifts when you purchase certain products. (Singaporeans love free gifts!) Others inform users that products from a certain brand are having discounts.

Unlike the “rojak” product lists on the main banner, users have a rough idea of what is on sale the very moment they see these small banners. These are banners that can get users to purchase products that did not originally have in mind. Wouldn’t it make more sense to put these at the very top of the page?

Issue #3: Overlapping Sub-Categories

Now, lets take a look at the category menu. For each category, we have multiple subcategories, all falling under one list. The problem is, many of them are overlapping, which confuses the user.

I also noticed that RedMart puts events as a subcategory. As seen in the image, under “Fruits & Vegetables” we have Fresh Tival, a fresh and frozen fair running from 21 to 27 August. Wouldn’t it be better if we put this as a banner? It will definitely get more attention as a banner than as a subcategory.

Issue #4: Missing Product Details

Without stating the ingredients list, RedMart isn’t making full use of the product details section on the product page. While the images do show ingredients and nutrition facts, the text is often too small. Isn’t this what the product details section is for? Why are you making your users strain their eyes?

There also seem to be some inconsistencies in the product details. Some do not have guarantees for expiry dates, while others do not even have a product description. The quality of product description also varies quite a bit. Seems like someone isn’t doing their job filling the product details up…

New Main Page Layout

Taking the issues above into consideration, I redid the main page. The top banner now shows the deals that are brand/product specific. By looking at this banner for Guinness, we immediately get the idea that Guinness is giving away free mooncakes if we purchase their beer. Very straightforward.

For the mobile UI, I removed the featured section and made the categories menu icons larger. The icons are now cartoonish, which is more pleasant to look at than the real-life icons before. (Excuse my poor art skills.) Also, they now have colored background, to stand out better from the white background.

The bottom navigation bar has been simplified to only 2 toggles. The “On Sale” page has been removed for the same reason as the featured section; no one is here to hunt for discount. Everything on the main page fits on a single screen, so users do not have to scroll to get to anything.

As for the desktop interface, I brought over the new icons and added a couple of extras. Since we have more space, I added a section to show how much the user is spending and saving on RedMart. This should provide them with a little bit of satisfaction when shopping on RedMart.

Discount Section in Categories & Search Results

Having a random list of discounted items is a bad idea. But the idea of a discounted list isn’t bad; You just need the right implementation. There are 2 places where it is logical to place a “discounted” section: the search results, as well as the product list pages for each (sub)category.

If you search for fish, you will see fishes on discount at the top of the list. Similarly. if you go to the “Fresh Seafood” category, seafood that are on discount will be placed higher up on the list. On these pages, the range of products filtered is fine enough to recommend you items you might want.

Featured Lists That Makes Sense

Unlike the mobile interface, I kept the featured product section on the desktop interface. But what we display on it will be different from before. Let’s categorize products into two categories: “needs”, which comprise of necessities plus regular groceries, and “wants”, which is everything extra.

Example of “needs”: Vegetables, fruits, meat, baby powder, frying pan, etc.…
Example of “wants”: Premium fruits, premium meat, snacks, chocolate, ice-cream, etc.…

For the new featured section, we will only show products under the “wants” categories, specifically those that are on discount. These are products that users originally had no intention to buy, but gets tempted once they see the deal. They can convince the user to buy more than what they need.

With this refinement, users will see less of RedMart Kitchen Towels and more of discounted Haagan-Dazs ice cream. There should only be one of such featured list to avoid clutter.

Reorganized Sub-Categories

Earlier, we talked about how the current sub-categories can be confusing for users. My solution would be to reduce and reorganize. For this example, I will be working on the “Fruit & Vegetables” category.

There are 3 overarching subcategories here: fruits, vegetables and mushrooms. The first two can further be split into “fresh” and “frozen”. I have also identified “organic” as a niche category. All other categories felt a little redundant, so I got rid of them. With that, we get the following menu:

Fruits & Vegetables
> Fruits
    > Fresh
    > Frozen
> Vegetables
    > Fresh
    > Frozen
> Mushrooms
> Organic

From a category with more than 10 subcategories, we reduced it to 4 subcategories, with two of the them each having 2 more subcategories. Depending on how the rest of the interface develop, we can turn the fresh/frozen and organic into a filter instead, simplifying things even further.

Other than the niche “organic” subcategory, all sub-categories do not have overlapping products.

Proper Template for Product Description

Solving the issue with poor product description is going to be easy but tedious. All we need is a template for product description, then feel it up for every product, if applicable. It is all about the effort. There are many ways to come up with the template, but here is one that I have created.

Brand & Country Manufactured
<Manufacturer Info>
Ingredients
<Ingredient List>
About Product
<Product Description> (Create one if not on packaging)
<Mass / Servings>
Freshness Guarantee
<Expiry Date Guarantee>
