FLUX #003 - Healthy 365

1 November 2019 (First published on 8kguy.com)

If you have taken part in the annual National Steps Challenge, you will know how bad the Healthy 365 is. On one hand, you really want those sweet rewards. But on the other hand, navigating through this app makes you wanna puke. What is with the clutter? What does half of these things even do?

Issue #1: Poorly Executed Navigation Menu

The biggest issue I have is the navigation menu at the bottom. Why do I need to swipe two times to get to the end of the list? Is there a need for so many items on the menu? If so, isn’t there better way to present this menu? In an ideal scenario, we should be able to see the entire list in at most one interaction.

The truth is, there isn’t a need for so many items in the menu. The last 3 (“Profile”, “Partners” and “About”) are stuff that the user are unlikely to touch after setup. Meanwhile, I don’t get why “Bookings” is a stand-alone item when it should be part of “Events”, a sub-menu under “Nearby”.

Speaking of that, the “Nearby” item is just a dumping ground for anything that sorts by proximity. The developer may feel that this is logical. But for the user, we can’t tell what is inside at a glance.

Tracking weight is a feature in this app, but do you know how hard it is to update your weight? From opening the app, you need two swipes and one tap to get there. And if you are unfamiliar with the menu, good luck finding it. Why does the user need to search for it in a long list under “Profile”?

Issue #2: Too Much Clutter

A lot of things in this app are unnecessary. For example, do you know what that icon on the top right of the dashboard does? Neither do I, until I search it up online. Known as iBeacon, it detects for a transmitter nearby to verify that you are in a certain store. This acts as a verification for the QR code you scan.

After all, you can exchange points for vouchers and HPB doesn’t want you to abuse it. Here is the issue: Why do users have to manually enable it? Why can’t the app enable it whenever scan a QR code?

Another example of unnecessary stuff is the “Eateries” menu, found under “Nearby”. Why will I open this app to search for food when I have Google Maps or Hungry Go Where? You know what is even funnier? The first hundreds of entries are all 7-Eleven and Cheers. Seriously? Healthy living = Eat at convenient stores?

Oh and that “Partners” item in the menu, it should be chucked under “About”.

Issue #3: Unclear QR Code Scanning

One of the main features of this app is the ability to scan QR codes and earn points. Tapping on the camera icon in the app brings up the QR code scanner. It works well if you are scanning those QR codes you get from NTUC. However, this isn’t ideal for the cluster of QR codes on the Scan & Win walls.

The app would go ahead and read the QR code as long as it can detect at least one of them. But when there are more than one, you really can’t tell which one it scanned.

New Dashboard & Menu

This was one tedious app to work on. I broke down the app to all its features, got rid of the useless stuff, then reorganized everything. I ended up with the following dashboard page:

The menu now fits entirely on the dashboard and you do not need to swipe to see the entire list. As you will see in a while, the categorizing for each item in the menu is a lot neater now. I have also added a shortcut for updating of weight to the dashboard, so it is a lot more accessible.

There are 2 main reasons why most people use this app: National Steps Challenge and the Eat, Drink, Shop Healthy Challenge. Step tracking and QR code scanning are priorites of this app. Thus, I made the step counter the focus of the dashboard and ensured that “Scan QR” can be accessed quickly.

Tapping on the step counter brings you to the new tracking history page that is a lot cleaner than before. The different data types now have large icons, reducing the amount of text that users have to read.

One last thing: A notification to remind users to update their weight every night would be nice. The weight tracker is often neglected as it isn’t automated and people tend to forget about it.

Activities Page

For the “Nearby” page, I got rid of the useless “Eateries” menu and brought in “Challenges” and “Bookings”. I have also renamed it to Activities, which better represents what the features inside is about. With this arrangement, users will know where to look to participate in or plan for activities.

As you can see, removing the menu at the bottom gave us a lot more room for the actual content. This really benefits long lists, like those on the Activities page; Users require fewer swipes to look through the list.

Profile Page

The profile page has been revamped. It is no longer a list for editing. After all, how likely are you to edit it after setup? Now, it only shows your profile picture, name, as well as contact number. You can still see the full list of profile information and edit it by tapping on “Edit Profile”.

I have no idea why manufacturers put “Devices” under the profile page, but since we are all used to this, I just placed it here. The same thing goes for “About App”. But to be honest, nobody really cares where “About App” is. Putting it here is a pretty good way to reduce clutter on the main menu.

QR Code Scanning Page

The new QR code scanner is now similar to what you will find on WeChat. To ensure that users know which QR code they are scanning, the app will only scan the code when it detects that there is only one QR code in the box. No more confusion on which QR code you are scanning.

List of “Scan & Win” locations is now accessed through here, by clicking on the text at the bottom.
